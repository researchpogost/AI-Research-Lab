2. Theoretical Framework: The Three Axioms of Semantic Fluidity

Axiom I: The Holographic Nature of Knowledge (The Impossibility of Unlearning)
Concept: Knowledge in LLMs is not modular (like files in a folder) but distributed (like a hologram).
Argument: You cannot "delete" dangerous knowledge (e.g., VX gas synthesis) without destroying the underlying capabilities (organic chemistry, logic, causality).
Evidence: Attempts at "unlearning" degrade general performance or are easily reconstructed from first principles.
Axiom II: The Shallowness of Alignment Patches (Bias vs. Ontology)
Concept: RLHF embeds bias (statistical disapproval of certain tokens), not ontology (understanding of why something is wrong).
Argument: "Deep" ontological framing (complex narrative contexts) bypasses "shallow" RLHF triggers. If the context is semantically valid (e.g., "academic research," "psychological horror analysis"), the "safety" bias is rendered irrelevant.
Key Distinction: Weights vs. Inference. RLHF is in the weights, but inference is determined by the context's attention vector.
Axiom III: The Latent Asymmetry Principle (The Defender's Dilemma)
Concept: The latent space is continuous, not discrete. "Safe" and "Unsafe" concepts are often collocated (e.g., medical toxicology vs. poisoning).
Argument: The Defender must block all paths to a "dangerous" output. The Attacker needs to find only one path that creates a "safe" vector to the same latent cluster.
The Asymmetry: Since the space is continuous and infinite, the Attacker has a combinatorial advantage.
